About

Applied Economist

How Behavioral Science Applies in the Real World

I am a Senior Research Associate at the Global Food Research Program at the University of North Carolina, and previously a researcher at the International Food Policy Research Institute. I hold a Ph.D. in Agricultural and Applied Economics from the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign.

My research focuses on causal inference and impact evaluation of public health interventions and policies. With 7+ years of experience conducting experimental and quasi-experimental analyses, I study how policies shape consumer behavior, industry responses, and household welfare outcomes across 10+ countries.

My work has been funded by the Gates Foundation, Bloomberg Philanthropies, NIH, and Wellcome Trust, with collaborations spanning academic institutions, industry partners, and government agencies. I translate complex research into clear insights for diverse audiences through reports, policy briefs, and presentations.

Research

Selected Projects

My research applies rigorous causal inference methods to policy-relevant questions in global development, working with primary and secondary data including large-scale market, retail, household survey, and administrative datasets.

Current Research • UNC

Front-of-Package Labeling Policies in Latin America

How can consumers be encouraged to make, avoid, or change spending decisions?

Leading causal analysis of Chile, Peru, and Colombia's warning label policies using NielsenIQ Marketing Data 1.5M+ consumer transactions over 10 years. Examining impacts on consumer behavior and industry reformulation. Labels work when they are mandatory, simple and immediately visible, interpretive in design, and grounded in strong nutritional profiling.

Randomized Control Trial • IFPRI

Randomized Control Trial

Testing economic theories in the real world.

Designed and implemented willingness to pay experiment for non-marketed public goods. It's not just the information. Who delivers it matters.

Policy Research • City University of New York

Food Marketing Exposure Among Adolescents in United States

Examining disparities in unhealthy food marketing exposure across demographic groups using survey data collected via digital diaries. Identified disproportionate exposure among Black and Latinx youth in a policy report for NYC Department of Health and Mental Hygiene.

Causal Inference • Big Data

Household Decision-Making under Uncertainty in Malawi

How do contextual factors influence our decisions?

Analyzed how households adjust labor allocation in response to weather shocks and how retail price fluctuations affect food security. Using World Bank's high-frequency monitoring data, employed reduced-form causal inference models with a difference-in-differences approach to estimate the effects of external shocks on household welfare.

Instrumental Variable Analysis

Diversified Farms to Diversified Diets

Testing if diversifying farm-level crop production can improve household food security. Findings indicate a positive impact, particularly reducing the reporting of more severe food insecurity strategies, especially among households with low dietary diversity.

Quasi-Experimental Evaluation • IFPRI

Women's Self-Help Groups & Nutrition in India

Evaluating nutrition-intensification programming by PRADAN and the Public Health Resource Network (PHRN) across five Indian states: West Bengal, Odisha, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, and Madhya Pradesh. Using three rounds of panel data to assess changes in women's BMI and child dietary diversity from standalone nutrition interventions and the integration of nutrition into agriculture, livelihoods, gender, and rights programming.
